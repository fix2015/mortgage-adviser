Hi {{first_name}},

"How much can I borrow?" — it's the first question everyone asks. And most people get it wrong.

Here's how it actually works.

**The income multiple**

Most lenders use a multiple of your annual income — typically between 4 and 4.5 times your salary. Some specialist lenders stretch to 5 or even 5.5 times for certain professions.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

| Annual Income | × 4.0 | × 4.5 | × 5.0 |
|--------------|-------|-------|-------|
| **£30,000** | £120,000 | £135,000 | £150,000 |
| **£50,000** | £200,000 | £225,000 | £250,000 |
| **Joint £80,000** | £320,000 | £360,000 | £400,000 |

That's a difference of up to **£80,000** on a joint income — just by finding the right lender.

**But income isn't the whole picture**

Lenders also look at:

- **Your monthly outgoings** — credit cards, car finance, student loans, childcare
- **Your deposit size** — a bigger deposit unlocks better rates and higher borrowing
- **Your employment type** — permanent, contract, self-employed all get treated differently
- **Your credit history** — not just your score, but the detail behind it

A broker might glance at your payslip and give you a ballpark. Our AI analyses your actual documents — payslips, bank statements, spending patterns — and matches you against lenders who are most likely to approve you at the best rate.

**The difference matters.** On a £250,000 mortgage over 25 years, even a 0.5% rate improvement saves you roughly **£15,000** in interest.
