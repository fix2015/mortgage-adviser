# How Much Can I Borrow? UK Mortgage Affordability Guide 2026

**Wondering how much you can borrow for a mortgage?** You are not alone — it is one of the most common questions first-time buyers and home movers ask. The answer depends on your income, your outgoings, your deposit, and the lender you choose. In this guide, we break down exactly how mortgage affordability works in the UK in 2026, with real salary examples, so you can walk into the process with confidence.

Whether you earn £25,000 or £100,000, whether you are buying alone or with a partner, this guide will give you a clear picture of what you could realistically borrow — and what steps you can take to maximise your borrowing power.

## How Do Lenders Decide How Much You Can Borrow?

When you apply for a mortgage, lenders do not simply look at your salary and hand you a number. They run a detailed **mortgage affordability assessment** that considers your full financial picture. Here is what goes into it:

- **Your gross annual income** (before tax)
- **Regular outgoings** — credit card payments, loans, childcare costs, car finance
- **Living expenses** — food, utilities, transport, subscriptions
- **Your deposit size** and the resulting loan-to-value (LTV) ratio
- **Your credit history** — missed payments, defaults, or CCJs
- **The interest rate environment** — lenders stress-test your ability to pay if rates rise

The goal is simple: the lender wants to be confident you can keep up with repayments, not just today, but if circumstances change.

## Income Multiples: The Quick Rule of Thumb

The fastest way to estimate how much you can borrow for a mortgage is the **income multiple** method. Most UK lenders offer between **4 and 4.5 times your gross annual salary**.

Some lenders stretch to **5 or even 5.5 times income** in certain circumstances — for example, if you are a high earner, have a large deposit, or work in specific professions such as medicine or law.

Here is how the multiples work in practice:

| Annual Salary | 4x Multiple | 4.5x Multiple | 5x Multiple |
|---------------|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| £25,000 | £100,000 | £112,500 | £125,000 |
| £30,000 | £120,000 | £135,000 | £150,000 |
| £40,000 | £160,000 | £180,000 | £200,000 |
| £50,000 | £200,000 | £225,000 | £250,000 |
| £60,000 | £240,000 | £270,000 | £300,000 |
| £80,000 | £320,000 | £360,000 | £400,000 |
| £100,000 | £400,000 | £450,000 | £500,000 |

**Important:** These figures are starting points only. Your actual borrowing amount will be adjusted up or down based on your full affordability assessment.

## Practical Examples: What Can You Actually Borrow?

Let us put some real numbers to this. These examples assume a clean credit history and standard lender criteria.

### On a £30,000 Salary

On a £30,000 salary, most lenders will offer you between **£120,000 and £135,000**. With a 10% deposit of £15,000, you could be looking at properties in the **£135,000 to £150,000** range.

If you are a first-time buyer, you may also benefit from government schemes or lender incentives that could stretch your budget a little further. At this salary level, monthly repayments on a £130,000 mortgage over 30 years at 4.5% would be roughly **£659 per month**.

### On a £50,000 Salary

Earning £50,000 puts you in a stronger position. Expect to borrow between **£200,000 and £250,000** depending on the lender and your outgoings. With a £25,000 deposit (10%), you could target properties up to **£275,000**.

Monthly repayments on a £225,000 mortgage over 25 years at 4.5% would be approximately **£1,251 per month**. If you have minimal debts and a larger deposit, some lenders may offer you closer to 5 times your income.

### Joint Income of £80,000

Buying with a partner changes the picture significantly. On a combined income of £80,000, you could borrow between **£320,000 and £400,000**. With a 15% deposit of £60,000, your property budget could reach **£460,000**.

This opens up significantly more of the UK property market. Monthly repayments on a £360,000 mortgage over 30 years at 4.5% would be around **£1,824 per month**.

## What Is a Mortgage Affordability Stress Test?

Income multiples give you a rough figure, but lenders go deeper with **affordability stress tests**. Since the Mortgage Market Review (MMR) rules were introduced, every lender must check that you could still afford repayments if interest rates rise.

Typically, lenders will test your affordability at **the current rate plus 1-3 percentage points**. So if you are applying for a mortgage at 4.5%, they might check whether you could cope at 6.5% or even 7.5%.

This is why two people earning the same salary can be offered very different mortgage amounts. If one person has £500 a month in car finance and credit card payments, their stress-tested affordability will be considerably lower than someone with no debts at all.

### What Counts Against You in Stress Tests

- **Personal loans and car finance** — even small monthly commitments reduce your borrowing power
- **Credit card balances** — lenders typically assume a minimum repayment percentage, even if you pay in full each month
- **Student loan repayments** — these are factored into your outgoings
- **Childcare costs** — nursery fees, after-school clubs
- **Regular subscriptions** — gym memberships, streaming services, phone contracts
- **Gambling transactions** — even occasional betting can raise red flags with some lenders

## How Your Deposit Affects Your Mortgage

Your deposit determines your **loan-to-value (LTV) ratio**, which is one of the most important factors in your mortgage application. The LTV is the percentage of the property price you need to borrow.

**Example:** If you are buying a £250,000 property with a £25,000 deposit, your LTV is 90%.

Here is why your deposit matters:

### Better Rates with Lower LTV

| LTV | Typical Rate Range (2026) | Deposit on £250,000 Property |
|-----|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 95% | 5.0% – 5.8% | £12,500 |
| 90% | 4.3% – 5.0% | £25,000 |
| 85% | 4.0% – 4.7% | £37,500 |
| 80% | 3.8% – 4.4% | £50,000 |
| 75% | 3.5% – 4.2% | £62,500 |
| 60% | 3.2% – 3.8% | £100,000 |

The difference between a 95% LTV and a 75% LTV could save you **hundreds of pounds per month** in repayments and **tens of thousands over the life of your mortgage**.

### First-Time Buyer Mortgage Options

If you are a first-time buyer, you may have access to schemes that help with smaller deposits:

- **Lifetime ISA** — save up to £4,000 per year with a 25% government bonus (up to £1,000 per year), usable on properties up to £450,000
- **Shared Ownership** — buy a share of a property (usually 25-75%) and pay rent on the rest
- **95% LTV mortgages** — widely available again in 2026, though rates are higher
- **Family-assisted mortgages** — parents can use their savings or property as security to help you access better rates

## What Do Lenders Look At Beyond Income?

Your salary is just one piece of the puzzle. Here is the full picture lenders consider:

### Credit Score and History

Lenders check your credit file with one or more of the three main UK credit reference agencies — Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion. They are looking for:

- **Missed or late payments** in the last six years
- **Defaults or CCJs** (County Court Judgements)
- **Bankruptcy or IVAs** (Individual Voluntary Arrangements)
- **How much existing credit you have** and how much you use
- **Whether you are on the electoral roll** at your current address
- **Stability** — how long you have been at your address and in your job

A strong credit score does not just help you get approved — it can unlock better rates and higher income multiples.

### Employment Type and Stability

Lenders prefer applicants who have been in their current role for at least **six months to a year**. If you have recently changed jobs, some lenders will still consider you — especially if you are in the same industry or have passed your probation period.

### Age and Mortgage Term

Most lenders want the mortgage repaid before you reach **70 to 75 years old**, though some will lend into retirement if you can demonstrate sufficient pension income. A shorter maximum term means higher monthly payments, which reduces how much you can borrow.

## Self-Employed Mortgages: How Borrowing Differs

If you are self-employed, getting a mortgage is absolutely possible, but the process works a little differently. Lenders typically need:

- **Two to three years of accounts** or SA302 tax returns
- **An accountant's reference** or certified accounts
- Evidence of **consistent or growing income**

### How Lenders Calculate Self-Employed Income

For **sole traders**, lenders usually take your **net profit** (after expenses, before tax) as your income figure.

For **limited company directors**, it gets more nuanced. Some lenders only count your **salary plus dividends**. Others will consider **salary plus share of net profit**, which can result in a significantly higher borrowing figure.

**Example:** A limited company director taking a £12,000 salary and £40,000 in dividends would have an assessed income of £52,000 with most lenders — giving borrowing power of £208,000 to £260,000. But a lender that considers retained profits might assess income at £70,000 or more, pushing borrowing to £280,000-£350,000.

If your income varies significantly year to year, lenders may **average your last two or three years** of earnings. Some will take the latest year if it is the highest — others will take the lowest. Choosing the right lender makes a real difference when you are self-employed.

## How to Increase Your Mortgage Borrowing Power

If the numbers are not quite where you need them, here are proven strategies to increase how much you can borrow:

### 1. Pay Down Existing Debts

This is the single most effective thing you can do. Clearing a car finance deal of £300 per month could add **£50,000 or more** to your borrowing capacity. Even paying off credit cards helps, as lenders factor in the credit limit, not just the balance.

### 2. Save a Larger Deposit

A bigger deposit does not just mean borrowing less — it means accessing **lower interest rates** through a better LTV band. Dropping from 90% to 85% LTV can save you a significant amount over the mortgage term.

### 3. Extend Your Mortgage Term

Borrowing over 30 or 35 years instead of 25 reduces your monthly payments, which means you pass the affordability stress test more comfortably. You will pay more interest overall, but it gets you on the ladder.

### 4. Choose the Right Lender

Not all lenders calculate affordability the same way. Some are more generous with overtime, bonuses, or commission income. Others offer higher multiples for certain professions. A broker — or a smart tool — can help you find the best match for your circumstances.

### 5. Buy with a Partner

Two incomes almost always mean more borrowing power. If you are buying with a partner, friend, or family member, your combined income is used for the affordability calculation.

### 6. Reduce Your Outgoings

In the three to six months before applying, try to reduce visible spending. Cancel unused subscriptions, avoid taking on new credit, and keep your bank statements tidy. Lenders do review your spending patterns.

### 7. Consider Additional Income

Many lenders will consider supplementary income sources:

- **Overtime and bonuses** — some lenders count 50-100% of regular overtime
- **Rental income** — if you own another property, some of the rental income may count
- **Second jobs** — regular part-time employment alongside your main role
- **Investment income** — dividends, interest, or other regular investment returns

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much can I borrow on a £30,000 salary?

On a £30,000 salary, most UK lenders will offer between £120,000 and £135,000 using standard income multiples of 4 to 4.5 times your salary. Your actual amount may vary depending on your debts, credit score, and deposit size. Some specialist lenders may offer up to £150,000 if you have an excellent financial profile and a larger deposit.

### How much can I borrow on a £50,000 salary?

On a £50,000 salary, you can typically borrow between £200,000 and £250,000. With minimal outgoings and a good credit score, some lenders offering 5 times income could stretch this to £250,000. Adding a 10-15% deposit means you could realistically look at properties in the £225,000 to £290,000 range.

### Do all lenders use 4.5 times income?

No. While 4 to 4.5 times income is the standard range, some lenders offer up to 5 or 5.5 times income in specific circumstances. Higher multiples are typically available to higher earners (usually above £50,000-£75,000), those with larger deposits, or professionals in certain careers such as doctors, lawyers, or accountants.

### Can I get a mortgage if I am self-employed?

Yes, self-employed applicants can absolutely get a mortgage. You will typically need at least two years of accounts or SA302 tax returns, a good credit history, and evidence of stable or growing income. Some lenders specialise in self-employed mortgages and may be more flexible with how they assess your income.

### How does my deposit affect how much I can borrow?

Your deposit determines your loan-to-value (LTV) ratio. While a larger deposit does not directly increase the amount a lender will offer you (that is based on income), it does unlock better interest rates and makes you a more attractive borrower. Some lenders may also offer higher income multiples at lower LTV ratios.

### What is a mortgage affordability stress test?

A stress test is when a lender checks whether you could still afford your mortgage repayments if interest rates increased — typically by 1 to 3 percentage points above the rate you are applying for. This is a regulatory requirement designed to protect borrowers from getting into financial difficulty if rates rise.

### How can I increase how much I can borrow?

The most effective strategies are: paying down existing debts (especially car finance and credit cards), saving a larger deposit, extending your mortgage term to 30 or 35 years, buying with a partner, reducing visible outgoings before applying, and choosing a lender whose criteria suit your circumstances.

### Does overtime count towards mortgage affordability?

It depends on the lender. Many UK lenders will consider regular overtime, typically counting between 50% and 100% of your average overtime earnings over the past one to two years. You will usually need to show that the overtime is a consistent, ongoing part of your role rather than a one-off.
