## 2. Mortgage Affordability

### Income Multiples

| Lender Type | Typical Multiple | Maximum Multiple |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| High street banks | 4.0x - 4.5x income | 5.0x (some criteria) |
| Building societies | 4.0x - 4.75x income | 5.5x (professionals) |
| Specialist lenders | 4.0x - 5.0x income | 6.0x (high earners) |

### Affordability Stress Testing

Lenders must stress-test affordability at a higher rate:
- Typically the lender's SVR + 1-2%, or a minimum of around 6-7%
- Ensures borrowers can afford payments if rates rise
- This is the main reason people cannot borrow as much as the income multiple suggests

### Income Types Accepted

| Income Type | Typical Treatment | Key Requirements |
|-------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Employed (PAYE) | 100% of basic + variable % of overtime/bonus | Latest 3 months payslips, P60 |
| Self-employed (sole trader) | Average of last 2-3 years net profit | SA302s + tax year overviews |
| Self-employed (Ltd company) | Salary + dividends (or net profit for some lenders) | 2-3 years accounts, SA302s |
| Contract workers | Annualised day rate or contract value | 12+ months contracting history |
| Rental income (BTL) | 125-145% rental coverage at stress rate | Rental valuation / AST |
| Benefits | Some accepted (child benefit, tax credits, disability) | Varies by lender |
| Pension income | 100% accepted | Pension statement |
| Maintenance payments | Some lenders accept | Court order or CSA agreement |

### Key Expenditure Considerations

- Existing credit commitments (loans, credit cards, car finance)
- Childcare costs
- School fees
- Ground rent and service charges
- Council tax
- Utilities and living costs (ONS-based estimates)
- Student loan repayments (Plan 1, 2, 4, or 5)

## 3. Loan-to-Value (LTV) Tiers

### LTV and Deposit Requirements

| LTV | Deposit Required | Rate Impact | Availability |
|-----|-----------------|-------------|-------------|
| 60% | 40% of property value | Best rates available | Wide |
| 75% | 25% of property value | Good rates | Wide |
| 80% | 20% of property value | Slightly higher rates | Wide |
| 85% | 15% of property value | Higher rates | Good |
| 90% | 10% of property value | Noticeably higher rates | Good |
| 95% | 5% of property value | Highest rates, limited options | Limited |

### Deposit Sources Accepted

- Personal savings
- Gift from family (gift letter required, no repayment expected)
- Inheritance
- Sale of existing property
- Help to Buy ISA / Lifetime ISA bonus
- Builder incentives (up to 5% typically accepted)
- Equity from existing property (remortgage)

### Deposit Sources NOT Typically Accepted

- Loans (unless disclosed and factored into affordability)
- Credit cards
- Gambling winnings (AML concerns)
- Cryptocurrency (some lenders now accept with audit trail)
- Cash with no clear source

## 4. Mortgage Types Explained

### Repayment vs Interest-Only

| Feature | Repayment | Interest-Only |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| Monthly payment | Higher | Lower |
| Capital reduction | Yes, every month | No -- full balance due at end |
| Total interest paid | Less over the term | More over the term |
| Availability | Standard for residential | Requires repayment vehicle |
| Typical use | Residential purchases | BTL, high-value residential |

### Repayment Vehicle Requirements (Interest-Only)

For residential interest-only mortgages, lenders typically require:
- Minimum equity of 50% (some 25%)
- A credible repayment strategy (sale of property, investments, pension, endowment)
- Regular review of the repayment plan

### Fixed Rate Mortgages

- Rate locked for 2, 3, 5, 7, or 10 years
- Provides payment certainty
- Early Repayment Charges (ERCs) during the fixed period
- Typically reverts to SVR at end of fixed term
- Most popular choice in the UK (approximately 80% of new mortgages)

### Tracker Mortgages

- Rate = Bank of England base rate + a set margin
- Rate moves up or down with base rate changes
- Often no ERCs (or lower ERCs)
- Good when rates are expected to fall
- Can be lifetime or for a set period

### Discount Variable Mortgages

- Rate = Lender's SVR minus a set discount
- SVR is set by the lender (not directly linked to base rate)
- Less predictable than trackers
- Can be good short-term options

### Offset Mortgages

- Savings balance "offsets" against mortgage balance
- Only pay interest on the net balance
- Savings remain accessible but earn no interest
- Tax-efficient for higher-rate taxpayers
- Example: GBP 200,000 mortgage with GBP 50,000 savings = interest charged on GBP 150,000

## 5. Government Schemes

### Lifetime ISA (LISA)

- Save up to GBP 4,000 per year
- Government adds 25% bonus (up to GBP 1,000 per year)
- Must be aged 18-39 to open
- Property must be GBP 450,000 or less
- Must be a first-time buyer
- Must have been open for at least 12 months before using
- Penalty for withdrawal for non-property purposes (25% of total)

### First Homes Scheme

- New-build homes sold at 30-50% discount to market value
- Must be a first-time buyer (or meet other criteria)
- Household income under GBP 80,000 (GBP 90,000 in London)
- Property price cap: GBP 250,000 after discount (GBP 420,000 in London)
- Discount stays with the property in perpetuity
- Must use a mortgage for at least 50% of the purchase price

### Shared Ownership

- Buy a share (25-75%) of a property, pay rent on the rest
- Available through housing associations
- Can "staircase" (buy more shares) over time
- Household income under GBP 80,000 (GBP 90,000 in London)
- Available for first-time buyers, previous homeowners who cannot afford now, and existing shared owners
- Repairs responsibility: 100% from day one (since 2021 model)

### Right to Buy / Right to Acquire

- Council tenants can buy their home at a discount
- Maximum discount: GBP 96,000 (GBP 127,900 in London) for 2024/25
- Must have been a public sector tenant for 3+ years
- Discount must be repaid if property sold within 5 years (on a sliding scale)

### Help to Buy Equity Loan (CLOSED)

- Scheme closed to new applications on 31 October 2022
- Final completions by 31 March 2023
- Existing loans: interest-free for first 5 years, then 1.75% rising with CPI + 2%
- Must be repaid on sale or at end of mortgage term

### Mortgage Guarantee Scheme

- Government guarantees part of the mortgage for 95% LTV lending
- Encourages lenders to offer 95% LTV products
- Extended to June 2025 (check current status)
- Borrower deals directly with the lender -- no separate application

## 8. Remortgaging Guide

### When to Remortgage

- Fixed rate / discount period ending (most common reason)
- Want to release equity for home improvements
- Consolidate debts (be cautious -- turns unsecured debt into secured)
- Property value has increased (lower LTV = better rates)
- Change from interest-only to repayment
- Circumstances have changed (income increase, credit score improvement)

### Remortgage Timeline

| Stage | Timing | Action |
|-------|--------|--------|
| Research | 3-6 months before deal ends | Compare rates, check affordability |
| Application | 3-4 months before | Apply with new lender or product transfer |
| Valuation | 2-3 months before | Lender values the property |
| Legal work | 2-3 months before | Conveyancer handles transfer |
| Completion | On or before deal end date | New mortgage starts |

### Product Transfer vs Full Remortgage

| Feature | Product Transfer | Full Remortgage |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Stay with same lender | Yes | No (new lender) |
| Legal fees | Usually none | GBP 1,000-2,000 (often free with deal) |
| Valuation | Usually none | Required (often free) |
| Speed | Days to weeks | 4-12 weeks |
| Rate options | Limited to current lender | Whole market |
| Release equity | Sometimes | Yes |
| Affordability assessment | Usually simplified | Full assessment |

### Early Repayment Charges (ERCs)

- Typically 1-5% of the outstanding balance
- Reduce each year during the fixed period
- Example: 5% in year 1, 4% in year 2, 3% in year 3, etc.
- Always calculate if the saving from a new rate outweighs the ERC
- Some products have no ERCs (usually trackers)

## 9. Buy-to-Let Mortgages

### Key Differences from Residential

| Feature | Residential | Buy-to-Let |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| Minimum deposit | 5% | 25% (typically) |
| Affordability test | Income-based | Rental coverage ratio |
| Interest type | Repayment or interest-only | Usually interest-only |
| Stress test rate | SVR + 1-2% | Typically 5.5% |
| Regulation | FCA regulated | Usually not FCA regulated |
| Tax relief on interest | N/A | Tax credit at 20% (Section 24) |

### Rental Coverage Ratio

- Most lenders require rent to be 125-145% of mortgage interest payments
- Calculated at a stress rate (typically 5.5%)
- Example: GBP 150,000 mortgage at 5.5% = GBP 8,250/year interest
  - At 125%: rent must be at least GBP 10,313/year (GBP 859/month)
  - At 145%: rent must be at least GBP 11,963/year (GBP 997/month)

### Tax Implications (Section 24)

- Mortgage interest is no longer deductible from rental profit
- Instead, a 20% tax credit is given on interest payments
- This particularly affects higher-rate taxpayers
- Can push landlords into a higher tax band on paper
- Many landlords have incorporated (limited company) as a result

### Portfolio Landlord Criteria (4+ properties)

- Additional stress testing across the entire portfolio
- Business plan may be required
- Experience in property management assessed
- Cash flow analysis across all properties
- Some lenders will not lend to portfolio landlords

## 10. Credit Score and Mortgage Applications

### Credit Score Factors

| Factor | Impact | Weight |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Payment history | Missed payments, defaults, CCJs | Very High |
| Credit utilisation | How much of available credit is used | High |
| Length of credit history | Older accounts are better | Medium |
| Types of credit | Mix of credit cards, loans, mortgage | Medium |
| Recent applications | Hard searches in last 6 months | Medium |
| Electoral roll | Being registered at current address | Medium |
| Financial associations | Linked to someone with poor credit | Low-Medium |

### Common Credit Issues and Mortgage Impact

| Issue | Impact on Mortgage | Typical Wait Period |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Missed payment (1) | Minor -- many lenders still lend | Must be satisfied |
| Missed payments (3+) | Significant -- limits lender options | 12-24 months |
| Default | Major -- specialist lenders only | 3-6 years (depends on lender) |
| CCJ (under GBP 500) | Significant | 12 months (if satisfied) |
| CCJ (over GBP 500) | Major | 3-6 years |
| IVA | Severe -- very limited options | Must be discharged + 3 years |
| Bankruptcy | Severe | Must be discharged + 3-6 years |
| Debt Management Plan | Moderate to significant | Must be completed + 12 months |
| Payday loans (recent) | Significant | 12+ months since last one |

### Improving Credit Score Before Application

1. Register on the electoral roll at current address
2. Pay all bills on time for at least 6 months
3. Reduce credit card balances to under 30% of limit
4. Do not apply for new credit in the 6 months before applying
5. Check credit report for errors and dispute any
6. Close unused credit accounts (if too many) or keep old ones open (for history)
7. Remove financial associations with people with poor credit
8. Avoid payday loans completely

## 11. The Mortgage Application Process

### Step-by-Step

| Step | Timeline | What Happens |
|------|----------|-------------|
| 1. Decision in Principle (DIP) | Day 1 | Soft credit check, indicative borrowing amount |
| 2. Full application | Day 1-7 | Submit all documents, hard credit check |
| 3. Valuation | Week 1-2 | Lender values the property |
| 4. Underwriting | Week 2-4 | Lender assesses all documentation |
| 5. Mortgage offer | Week 3-6 | Formal offer issued (valid 3-6 months) |
| 6. Legal work | Week 3-8 | Conveyancer handles searches, contracts |
| 7. Exchange | Week 6-12 | Contracts exchanged, completion date set |
| 8. Completion | Week 8-16 | Keys handed over, mortgage starts |

### Documents Typically Required

**Employed:**
- Last 3 months payslips
- Last P60
- Last 3 months bank statements
- Photo ID (passport or driving licence)
- Proof of address (utility bill, council tax)
- Proof of deposit (savings statements, gift letter)

**Self-Employed:**
- Last 2-3 years SA302s and tax year overviews
- Last 2-3 years accounts (ideally certified by accountant)
- Last 3-6 months business and personal bank statements
- Company accounts if limited company
- Photo ID and proof of address
- Proof of deposit

**Additional (if applicable):**
- Divorce decree / financial settlement
- Benefits award letters
- Pension statements
- Existing mortgage statement
- Rental agreements (for BTL)

## 12. Key Mortgage Calculations

### Monthly Payment Formula

```
Monthly Payment = P * [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n - 1]

Where:
P = Principal (loan amount)
r = Monthly interest rate (annual rate / 12)
n = Total number of payments (years * 12)
```

### Example Calculations

**GBP 200,000 mortgage at 4.5% over 25 years (repayment):**
- Monthly payment: GBP 1,112
- Total interest paid: GBP 133,584
- Total amount repaid: GBP 333,584

**GBP 200,000 mortgage at 4.5% over 25 years (interest-only):**
- Monthly payment: GBP 750
- Total interest paid: GBP 225,000
- Capital still owed at end: GBP 200,000

**Impact of rate changes on GBP 200,000 repayment mortgage (25 years):**

| Rate | Monthly Payment | Difference from 4% |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| 3.0% | GBP 948 | -GBP 107 |
| 3.5% | GBP 1,001 | -GBP 54 |
| 4.0% | GBP 1,055 | -- |
| 4.5% | GBP 1,112 | +GBP 57 |
| 5.0% | GBP 1,169 | +GBP 114 |
| 5.5% | GBP 1,228 | +GBP 173 |
| 6.0% | GBP 1,289 | +GBP 234 |

### Overpayment Benefits

- Most mortgages allow 10% overpayment per year without ERC
- Overpaying GBP 100/month on a GBP 200,000 mortgage at 4.5%:
  - Saves approximately GBP 26,000 in interest
  - Pays off mortgage approximately 4 years early
- Even small regular overpayments make a significant difference

## 13. Property Types and Mortgage Considerations

### Standard Properties

- Houses (detached, semi-detached, terraced) -- widely accepted
- Purpose-built flats -- widely accepted (check lease length)
- Converted flats -- accepted by most lenders (some restrictions)

### Non-Standard Construction

| Type | Mortgage Availability | Notes |
|------|---------------------|-------|
| Timber frame | Good | Most lenders accept modern timber frame |
| Steel frame | Moderate | Some lenders restrict; depends on type |
| Concrete (PRC) | Limited | Must have PRC certificate of structural completion |
| Thatched roof | Limited | Specialist lenders, higher insurance costs |
| Flat above commercial | Moderate | Some lenders restrict; depends on commercial use |
| Ex-local authority | Good | Most lenders accept; some LTV restrictions |
| Listed buildings | Moderate | Structural survey usually required |
| New build | Good | But watch for premium pricing and lease terms |

### Lease Length Requirements

- Most lenders require minimum 70-85 years remaining on lease at application
- Some require lease to extend beyond mortgage term + 30-40 years
- Short leases significantly affect property value and mortgageability
- Lease extension costs increase dramatically below 80 years (marriage value)
- Leasehold Reform Act changes may affect future requirements

### Cladding and Building Safety

- Post-Grenfell, flats in buildings over 11m require:
  - EWS1 form (External Wall System fire safety assessment)
  - Or building safety certificate
- Buildings with unsafe cladding may be unmortgageable until remediated
- Government Building Safety Fund covers some remediation costs
- Leaseholders in buildings over 11m should not pay for remediation (Building Safety Act 2022)
